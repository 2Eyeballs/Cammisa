Performance exists in time. It cannot be held, archived, or replayed exactly as it occurred. Yet we document it obsessively—through video, photography, written description—trying to capture something that is fundamentally ephemeral.

## The Problem with Documentation

When we document performance, we inevitably lose something. The camera chooses what to see. The description interprets rather than transmits. The archive becomes a pale shadow of the living act.

But perhaps that's not a problem. Perhaps it's the point.

## Archive as New Work

I've been thinking about how archives themselves become artworks. The footage from 2016's *West Coast Civilization Shuffle Tour* exists differently now than it did in the moment. Viewed ten years later, it becomes something new—a document of a specific time, a record of collaboration, evidence of a practice that continues to evolve.

The incompleteness of documentation forces the viewer to imagine what they're missing. To fill in the gaps.

## Next Steps

I'm experimenting with how to present these works—how to make their incompleteness productive rather than disappointing. More on this soon.
